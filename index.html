The Story of the Internet

By: Stephen Bryant

CHAPTER ONE

Sputnik

The Soviet Union's launch of Sputnik 1 in 1957 shook American confidence, prompting Eisenhower to unify technological and military efforts, resulting in the creation of an agency that would later help develop the Internet.

CHAPTER TWO

The First Computer Network

In 1958, Eisenhower founded ARPA, initially focused on the space race, but soon turned to computing. Bob Taylor, leading computer projects, designed a network to connect scientists and, with the support of Wes Clark and Larry Roberts, developed the first IMPs, the basis for packet data. BBN built ARPAnet, launched in 1969 with the first message, marking the beginning of the Internet.

CHAPTER THREE

To the Internet

Frank Heart and his team at BBN secured the ARPAnet contract by developing Interface Message Processors (IMPs) crucial for computer connectivity. Despite a tight nine-month deadline, they used a Honeywell computer to create reliable IMPs, delivering the first to UCLA in September 1969. Resolving initial issues, they successfully transmitted the networkâ€™s first message, marking a historic milestone that laid the foundation for the Internet and revolutionized long-distance communication.

CHAPTER FOUR

The Personal Computer

In 1975, while at Harvard, Bill Gates and Paul Allen seized the opportunity to develop software for the Altair, the first personal computer, after recognizing its potential to revolutionize computing. Partnering with MITS, the Altair's creator Ed Roberts, they successfully demonstrated their BASIC interpreter and moved to Albuquerque to establish what became Microsoft. Meanwhile, inspired by the Altair, Steve Wozniak and Steve Jobs created the Apple II, which transformed personal computing by making it accessible to everyday users. This innovation boom eventually drew IBM into the market, leading to a partnership with Microsoft that forever changed the computing industry.

CHAPTER FIVE

The World Wide Web

In 1989, Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web at CERN, revolutionizing information sharing by enabling hypertext links between documents on different computers. He introduced innovations like URLs for unique addresses and HTML for formatting web pages. By 1991, the release of free browser software made the Web accessible to all, transforming the Internet from a tool for experts into a universal platform for creativity and communication.

CHAPTER SIX

Netscape

At just 23, Marc Andreessen revolutionized the Internet by creating the Mosaic browser, which made navigating the World Wide Web simple and visually engaging. He then co-founded Netscape Communications with funding from John Doerr, assembling a team of Mosaic developers to create Netscape Navigator. Known for its speed and user-friendly design, Navigator quickly became the dominant web browser, transforming online business and propelling Netscape to a historic public offering in 1995, marking it as one of the fastest-growing companies ever.

CHAPTER SEVEN

Yahoo! - A Guide to Everything

Yahoo! started as a project by Stanford students Jerry Yang and Dave Filo to organize the growing World Wide Web. Their human-curated directory quickly evolved into a comprehensive guide named "Yahoo!" in 1994. Embracing independence, they monetized their platform with advertising and added services like news and weather. By 1996, Yahoo! was a major Internet presence, valued at $850 million and shaping the future of online information.

CHAPTER EIGHT

The Future

The Internet's rapid evolution points to a future where it becomes even more central to daily life, empowering individuals with tools for storytelling, remote collaboration, and scientific contributions like SETI@Home. While innovations like free software challenge traditional industries, the rise in accessibility also brings risks such as cybercrime and information warfare. As it continues to reshape communication, work, and personal exploration, the Internet's potential to mobilize global participation in knowledge and discovery remains a defining feature of its transformative power.
